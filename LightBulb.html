Light bulb

In 1809, English inventor Humphry Davy created the first electric light. He had connected rods made of charcoal, a form of the chemical element carbon, to a battery with two wires. Although the light didn't last long, it showed that some materials became so hot that they glow when electricity passes through them. In the 1870s, American inventor Thomas Edison experimented with the electric light bulb. His version was an air-tight glass case containing a looped wire, called a filament, which was connected to a flow of electricity known as a current. If the current was strong enough, the filament glowed white-hot. Early filaments didn’t last long, but in 1879 he created a long-lasting filament made from carbon. The practical light bulb was born.

Edison tried 4,700 different materials to find the perfect filament, including hair from a beard!

    The filament was made of a single loop of carbon wire. The wire glowed when electricity passed through it. Longer-lasting filaments made from the chemical element tungsten were invented in 1910.

    The space inside the bulb is a partial vacuum, which means that air, including the gas oxygen, has been removed from it. The effect of removing oxygen was that the filament could get very hot without catching fire.

    Edison’s light bulb first went on sale in November 1880. It used a lot of electricity. To solve this problem, Edison invented the power station, which sent electricity to homes and businesses through wires.
